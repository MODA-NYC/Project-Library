1. Introduction


1a. Background

On January 24, 2019, Introduction 633 of 2018: Reporting of pay and employment equity data, passed into Local Law 18 of 2019 (LL18). Pursuant to this local law, each year, the Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS) collects data from agencies relevant to pay and employment of the City’s municipal workforce and makes this data available to the Mayor’s Office of Data Analytics (MODA). Upon receiving this data from DCAS, MODA is then responsible for three requirements:

  1. issue a report on the data, aggregated by pay band, with specific attributes; a. publish related code on the MODA Open Source Analytics Library;
  2. make a raw version of the data available to the City Council for 90 days; and
  3. conduct an analysis of the data, in conjunction with DCAS, to identify potential disparities based on gender, race, or other protected classes in terms of pay, employment rates, and retention rates.

MODA submits this Pay and Demographics Report on City of New York agency workforce pay and employment information in compliance with requirement #1 above for 2020. This is MODA’s first annual publication of the Pay and Demographics Report; the report is based on City employee data on December 31, 2018.

The first disparity analysis (#3 above) is due 90 days after the publication of the second annual report of aggregated pay and employment data in 2021.


1b. Impact on 2020 Report Publication

LL18 requires MODA to publish a report on the data by May 31st each year. However, this year, demands upon MODA to perform urgent Covid-19 data analytics work severely impacted the office staffing and operations during spring and summer of 2020. As a result, MODA’s publication of the 2020 Report occurred in October 2020.


1c. About MODA

The Mayor’s Office of Data Analytics (MODA) was established by executive order in 2013 and codified in the City Charter in 2018, and supports City agencies in applying strategic analytical thinking to data in order to deliver services more equitably and effectively, and to increase operational transparency. MODA’s mission is to use data analytics to help City agencies deliver services more efficiently, facilitate citywide data operations, and implement the City’s Open Data Law. MODA is committed to government transparency and sharing agency data of interest to New Yorkers publicly, as permitted by law, through the Open Data program. More information about MODA can be found at www.nyc.gov/analytics.


2. Agency Report Table


2a. Overview

The Agency Report Table1 aggregates pay and employment characteristics in accordance with LL18 requirements2. The Table covers over 180,000 employees from 36 City agencies and offices. The Table is a point-in-time snapshot of employees who were either active or on temporary leave (parental leave, military leave, illness, etc.) as of December 31st, 2018. The full list of agencies included in the mandate of LL18 is in Appendix B.

Each row in the Table represents a group of employees in a given agency with the same demographic, pay, and job category classification. To protect the privacy of employees, the sign “<5” is used instead of the actual number for groups of less than five (5) employees, in accordance with the Citywide Privacy Protection Policies and Protocols.3

There are two steps involved in the establishment of pay bands, the only attribute derived by MODA in the Table:

  • First, employees in the same employee status but whose pay is calculated based on different frequencies1, such as daily, hourly, annual or prorated, need to have their pay annualized or converted for pay comparison within the same employee status and;
  • Second, once the pay is standardized, a common pay band is assigned across the full-time, part-time and seasonal employees in order to aggregate employees.

Pay Annualization and Conversion

To support the establishment of pay bands, MODA standardized the pay of 9,136 employees (approximately 5%) out of more than 180,000 employees, due to the differences in the pay calculation frequency of the full-time (annual, daily, and hourly) and part-time (hourly and prorated annual) employees, by:

  • Annualizing the pay of 7,961 full-time employees whose pay is calculated based on a daily or hourly frequency using the conversion rate of 261 days/year or 1,827 hours/year, respectively, for the majority of these employees and;
  • Converting the salaries of 1,175 part-time employees to an hourly pay who are paid on prorated annual2 basis using the conversion rate of 1,958 hours/year for the majority of these employees.

For example, a full-time employee in the civil service title of Carpenter (EEO-4 Job Category: Skilled Craft) whose pay as of December 31, 2018 was $349.16 per day, had their pay annualized using the rate of 261 days per year3, which amounts to a salary of $91,130.76 per year. Similarly, a part-time employee in the civil service title of School Lunch Aide (EEO-4 Job Category: Service and Maintenance) who was paid $29,539.00 on a prorated annual basis, had their prorated salary converted into its hourly equivalent using the calculation rate of 1,958 hours per year, which amounts to a pay of $15.09 per hour.

Out of the 9,136 employees whose pay was standardized through the method above, MODA identified 79 part-time employees paid on a prorated annual basis where information about the number of hours they worked was unavailable in NYCAPS. As a result, MODA used the standard number of hours worked per year in these titles to convert the prorated salaries of these employees into their hourly rates.

MODA’s approach to standardize pay, with the full list of annualization and conversion rates in Appendix D, is consistent with City methodology; however no standard Citywide methodology exists to standardize pay for comparison.

Pay Band Selection Methodology

Once the pay is standardized into annual salaries or hourly rates, a challenge exists to select a common pay band across full-time, part-time, and seasonal employees whose pay is difficult to compare due to difference in hours worked.

For example, the pay of a part-time employee who earns $16.47 per hour as a Precinct Receptionist (EEO-4 Job Category: Administrative Support) would not be directly comparable to a full-time employee who earns $30,090.67 per year simply by assuming the part-time employee could work 1,827 hours a year4. Part-time employees are those that work fewer than 35 hours per week or are in civil service titles having no standard hours per week or days per year.

As LL18 requires the selection of a pay band for use, and to account for this potential misinterpretation of derived pay rates, the Agency Report Table maintains the usage of two separate pay band denominations: (1) per year pay band for full-time employees, and a separate (2) per hour pay band for part-time and seasonal employees. Based on MODA’s analysis across different pay bands to maximize the details on pay while ensuring anonymity, a $5,000 per year pay band is assigned to full-time employees and a separate $0.50 per hour pay band to part-time and seasonal employees.

MODA selected a pay band of $5,000 for full-time employees because there are five percentage points fewer rows with a “<5” sign than a pay band of $2,500, increasing the precision in the number of employees, particularly for several small and mid-sized agencies. Similarly, a $0.50 pay band for part-time and seasonal employees includes slightly fewer number of rows with the “<5” sign than a pay band of $0.25.

2d. Data Sources

NYCAPS: The primary data source for the Agency Report Table is the New York City Automated Personnel System (NYCAPS), a centralized automated personnel system for human resources professionals, managers and employees to access and manage personnel and benefits information. Employee Self-Service (ESS) is the online tool within NYCAPS that provides NYC employees with access to human resources, payroll, tax, City job applications and benefits information. Through ESS, employees may access and can voluntarily choose to update their personal information, including their gender, ethnicity, and race.

Datasets: The data provided by DCAS to MODA used in the creation of this Report provide a point-in-time snapshot for each full-time, part-time and seasonal employee active or on leave as of December 31, 2018. In addition to these datasets, MODA also received pay and employment information on seasonal employees active as of Summer 2018 to support representation of the range of seasonal employees employed by the City.

Minimum Wage: The data includes pay that is less than the current 2020 minimum wage as of 20181 for 13 part-time employees. The 13 employees are in College Aides and Community Service Aides titles (EEO-4 Job Category: Para-professionals), because the contractual collective bargaining increase was signed in 2019 with a salary effective date of December 31, 2018.

  1. NYS Minimum Wage. The minimum wage on December 31st, 2018, was $15 for large employers in New York City. ↩


3. 2021 Report

Next year’s Report will be released in May 2021, based on data that reflects a snapshot of City employees as of December 31st, 2019. This report will include the following two additional gender categories, which went into effect during the hiring process in 2019.

  • Non-binary (not female/woman or male/man)
  • Other (a gender not listed)

Additionally, per LL18 requirements, beginning after the issuance of the 2021 Report, MODA, in conjunction with DCAS, will conduct an annual analysis of LL18 Report data to identify potential disparities in the areas of pay, employment rates, and retention rates.

APPENDIX E: GLOSSARY

  • Citywide Privacy Protection Policies and Protocols - policies and protocols set forth by the City’s Chief Privacy Officer on the collection, retention, and disclosure of identifying information by City agencies and certain City contractors and subcontractors.

  • Data dictionary - a repository of information that accompanies a dataset, cataloging the names, definitions and attributes of its elements. The data dictionary for the Agency Report Table is available on the NYC Open Data Portal [link to add].

  • Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS) - The Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS) is a City agency with a mission to provide value-added and effective shared services to support the operations of New York City government.

  • Employee Self-Service (ESS) - Employee Self-Service (ESS) is the online tool within NYCAPS that provides NYC employees with easy access to human resources, payroll, tax, City job applications and benefits information.

  • EEO-4 Job Category- EEO-4 Job Category is used by the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to classify a group of employees with comparable job responsibilities at comparable levels within an organization. DCAS classifies each civil service title to one of the eight EEO-4 Job Categories as part of its legal mandate to submit EEO-4 reports every two years. For a detailed description of each job category, please see the EEO-4 Instruction Booklet.

  • Full-time, part-time and seasonal employees - Full-time employees include those who work a standard work week in a full-time title with a regular annual work schedule. In general, full-time employees work between 35 and 40 hours per week, and 261 days per year. Part-time employees are those who work fewer than 35 hours per week or are in titles having no standard hours per week or days per year. Seasonal employees such as lifeguards and many parks maintenance workers may work in either a part-time or full-time capacity. MODA reports data for seasonal employees separately from part-time and full-time employees per the Local Law 18 requirement.

  • Github - Github is a platform for collaborative software development that MODA uses to publish and share code.

  • Local Law 18 (LL18) - Local Law 18 of 2019 is a law that requires MODA to issue an annual public report on aggregated pay and employment data, grant access to City Council for a 90 day period each year, and conduct an annual disparity analysis of pay, employment rates, and retention rates beginning in 2021.

  • Agency Report Table - The Agency Report Table is the dataset component of MODA’s annual report of agency employment and pay data.

  • Mayor’s Office of Data Analytics (MODA) - The Mayor’s Office of Data Analytics (MODA) supports City agencies in applying strategic analytical thinking to data in order to deliver services more equitably and effectively, and to increase operational transparency.

  • NYC Open Data Portal - The NYC Open Data Portal hosts free public data published by New York City agencies and other partners.

  • New York City Automated Personnel System (NYCAPS) - a centralized automated personnel system for human resources professionals, managers and employees to access and manage personnel and benefits information.

  • Part-time Prorated Annual Employees - Employees paid on a prorated annual basis do not work full schedules and therefore their salary reflects the number of days that the employee worked.

  • Pay band - A pay band represents the minimum and maximum compensation range to assist with the grouping of employees. MODA selected two separate pay band denominations: $5,000 for full-time employees, and $0.50 for part-time and seasonal employees.

  • Pay calculation frequency - Indicates if the employee is paid on an annual, daily or hourly basis.
